Frequently Asked Questions

    What is NEOMODERN?
    NEOMODERN is a new photography experience. Customers not only step into the studio to create a piece of art from a picture they took, but also to surround themselves with inspiring photography both from peers, and from a collection of original masterpieces from modernist photographers like Ansel Adams, Henri Cartier-Bresson and Andre Kertesz.

    NEOMODERN intends to become an important brand for “high-end” amateur photography, reviving the hobbyist and printing industries. In execution, and at first, NEOMODERN has built a concierge photography salon producing museum-quality products for consumers from their personal photos. Walk-in customers get their photos optimized by professionals, printed and mounted, and even custom framed, all in under an hour, and with their direct participation.

    It’s not an errand. It’s an activity.
  • +

    How does it work?
    Customers bring their own digital photos into NEOMODERN that they want to turn into a beautiful piece of art. One of the photographic printing specialists (a “printmaster”) will work closely with them on the spot to edit, enhance and make the photo the best it can be. Once finalized, it’s printed right in store, mounted and professionally framed. Customers walk out of NEOMODERN with a museum-quality piece of art. It’s not a print service—you cannot leave with just a print (it’s not safe for the photo). And it’s not a frame store—the only works framed are the photos printed there. And it’s not just an art gallery—the photographs on the walls, whether by customers or by famous photographers, are primarily there to illustrate great work and inspire. Some gallery pieces may be for sale; it changes constantly.

    NEOMODERN will try to print from whatever kind of image file customers arrive with, but we will be candid about how large and sharp an image can be from that file. A 12 megapixel image, about the full size created from most smartphones after 2015, can usually be enlarged nicely to 8x10; but the best prints will come from cameras like:
    • Motorola Moto Z Droid Force
    • Samsung Galaxy S7; Samsung Galaxy S7 edge Sony Xperia XA Ultra
    • Apple iPhone 7; iPhone 7 Plus
    • Google Pixel, Pixel XL
    • And of course, all dSLRs...
    These, and pretty much all later models, will be able to make prints as large as 13x19 and have the image data that can be manipulated to improve them as required. The truth is, there are many factors that affect the quality of the image and NEOMODERN’s job is to help figure out how good it can be. Can older digital images be printed? Certainly. Through the gallery, customers will see how content and composition rule, and frequently resolution and “perfection” aren’t the limitations people think.

    What is “Image Optimization?"
    Printmasters work with customers to make images print as good as they can be, although the definition of ‘good’ is subjective and it really cannot be done without direct customer interaction. Printmasters use Adobe Lightroom® professional software to do image adjustments—which means they control darkroom-types of improvements: exposure, dynamic range, color balance. Particularly impactful is the more nuanced burning and dodging of parts of the image: to strengthen most images the exposures of different parts of the picture are brightened or darkened for effect. Most consumers have never seen their images professionally optimized.

    Important to note that this is different from “retouching” or literal image modification. Printmasters will not add or remove elements from an image, take out blemishes, wrinkles (or exes). This isn’t ‘Photoshopping’ pictures to make people look better or different. Image optimization follows journalistic guidelines of integrity: picture content is not changed.

    What problem is NEOMODERN solving?
    Most consumers don’t have professional skills or access to the required tools to improve or print their own photos. Filters and quick digital ‘improvements’ are fine for snapshots, but don’t have the quality and nuance of a concierge experience. AI may continue to make our snapshots better, but doesn’t replace the artistic process. Custom printing and custom framing are usually eparate processes and businesses—and both less-than-ideal: multi-step, slow, complicated, and expensive.

    NEOMODERN is streamlining the process and giving consumers easy access to professionals and quality tools and services. NEOMODERN is building a trusted brand that allows customers to self- educate about photographic art and how to improve their photos. At the same time it creates a fun and creative experience.

    In the simplest and most elegant way possible, NEOMODERN completes the camera to art pipeline to which few have access. Perhaps most importantly, however, is that NEOMODERN connects the millions of new photographers with the rich history of creative photography.

    What does “neomodern” mean?
    The modernist movement in photography began in the early 1900s, when 35mm film was invented and camera ownership became more mainstream. Today, a hundred years later, film is gone and yet everyone has a camera in their pocket. Still, it took until 2017 before camera phone images were of high enough quality that they could be printed the way film was traditionally printed.

    NEOMODERN believes that a photographic renaissance is just beginning, a new modernism— neomodernism—defined not by a nostalgic return to film and chemicals but by a revival of creative amateurs and hobbyists. Photography, now as ever, is about creating a beautiful physical object, a print, that will stand the test of time.

    What materials are used?
    Printing
    The photographs printed are technically “archival pigment prints” and are made using photographic pigments (as opposed to non-archival dyes). The word “archival” means that the materials being used will not degrade over time (or at least very very slowly). Other methods are notoriously prone to fading and other forms of deterioration. We print on professional Epson photographic inkjet printers and use the highest quality photo papers.

    Mounting The process of archival, museum-style mounting means that the photograph is not compromised in the process—it is neither glued nor taped down. This is the optimal way to treat important photographs; it allows the process to be easily undone, and for the prints to be safely removed, if needed. The window mats are hinged to the backing boards with linen tape, which is the way photographs are handled by art museums worldwide.

    Framing NEOMODERN uses custom wood frames, acid-free cotton mat boards and tapes, and UV- protecting acrylic to maximize the safety and longevity of the print. Additionally, NEOMODERN uses flexible pins to fix the print into the frame which makes it possible for the customer to easily release the mat and replace it with a different matted photo.

    Who is your target customer?
    Initially, NEOMODERN’s customers are semi-pros and advanced hobbyists who will frequent the studio for the convenience, speed and economics of getting this quality work done. But the real opportunity is in seeing what will happen when the service is made accessible for regular people. NEOMODERN’s customer, consequently, is any consumer, amateur photographer or hobbyist who wants to create a piece of art from a photo they took.

    Customers fall into four categories:
    • Family memory-keeper: “I use my camera to record family memories and milestones, and I’m frequently in charge of decisions about the decorations in the house.”
    • Hobbyist: “I enjoy the picture taking process and understand the functions of the manual controls of my camera. Historically I’ve used a DSLR, but increasingly use my smartphone.”
    • Advanced hobbyist: “I have advanced photography skills, but I don’t make a living from my photography. (But that was before I had such easy access to professional services.)”
    • Semi Pro: “I do occasionally work for hire, but not as a full time professional. I have a “regular” job...”

    What’s the market for this?
    To make a photo larger than 8x10, which is often the smallest size that photographic fine art is created, but also not uncommon, you’d either need analog film or a professional quality digital camera, good optics, a quality sensor and smart compression software. In short, you’d need a DSLR, that is, until the iPhone 7 was released. Only recently has “professional quality” been in the hands of virtually all consumers in their smartphone camera. At the peak year of analog film cameras, about 40 million were sold; last year more than 1.4 billion smartphones with cameras were sold—a market expansion of three orders of magnitude; as old phones are replaced, the market for quality photo services and activities will increase. This represents a new and very large market, and a new market opportunity that hasn’t fully been recognized.

    What’s in the gallery?
    The walls of NEOMODERN are covered with examples of inspiring photography from an exclusive private collection, as well as outstanding prints that are discovered from customers.

    The collection at NEOMODERN is selected from hundreds of modernist photographers including Ansel Adams, Henri Cartier-Bresson, Elliott Erwitt, Andre Kertesz, Harry Callahan and Alma Lavenson. The works will be constantly rotated through the gallery to best demonstrate modernist ways of seeing and photographing the people, events, and things around us. NEOMODERN believes that any person’s photographic skills will improve just by spending time looking at the masterpieces that surround them in the gallery.

    *A selection of the photography from customers and the collection will be noted if on sale.

    What is the NEOMODERN experience?
    You walk into NEOMODERN and the first thing you see are photographs. Beautiful prints. You could be in the SF MOMA or the DeYoung. You might recognize some of them, and then there are ones you don’t because they were made last week by people in the neighborhood. Not professionals, just regular people with cell phones. These prints are on the gallery walls next to prints by Cartier-Bresson.

    The first take away about NEOMODERN: it’s analog, but it’s not retro. While it demands that images must be printed in physical form to become photographs, it’s not wed to the technologies that created those images. There’s no fetishizing old cameras and celluloid film. You make prints at NEOMODERN, but there’s no darkroom. It’s the future of photography with a direct connection to the past. No other company is doing that or recognizes the singular importance of this.
